# Chapter 171: Awe

Li Qi made his way to the Minister of War's Manor and, bearing his gift, knocked on the door.

A sleepy-eyed gatekeeper answered. Carrying the anger of someone rudely awakened from his slumber, he grumbled at the door, "Who is it? Knocking on the door this late—do you have any manners at all?!"

"I am deeply ashamed to visit so late in the night, but I truly have critical military intelligence to report. I trouble you, brother, to notify Lord Minister of War. Tell him the White Rhino Army has an urgent military situation that requires his immediate decision!" Li Qi said solemnly from outside. He then passed a piece of broken gold through the peephole on the door. It was about a fifth of a tael of gold, worth roughly two thousand coins. To a gatekeeper, this was no small sum.

Money always paved the way for easier conversation. Seeing the gold, the gatekeeper swallowed his anger. He first snatched the gold through the peephole, weighed it in his hand, and then said, "You must be a soldier who's been stuck at the border pass and never been to Vast Yang before, right?"

"I indeed haven't been to Vast Yang before. Is there a problem?" Li Qi asked.

"If you're looking for the Minister of War, you have to go to Heavenly Peace Temple. How could you find him at the Minister of War's Manor? If you really have an urgent military report, head to Heavenly Peace Temple. Those senior monks are reasonable enough; they shouldn't try to stop you," the gatekeeper said.

"He's at Heavenly Peace Temple right now?!" Li Qi was stunned. He had heard as much from the street vendor, but to think it was actually true?

"Where else would he be? He hasn't been back here for a long time. If you really have an urgent military report, you'd better hurry. Do you know where Heavenly Peace Temple is? Need me to tell you?" the gatekeeper offered.

"That won't be necessary. I'll head over right now." With that, Li Qi turned and left.

"Go on, off with you! Hurry up, or you might end up waiting until tomorrow!" the gatekeeper urged, snapping the peephole shut.

Li Qi left and returned to the streets.

Heavenly Peace Temple had already become a landmark of Vast Yang City. He had learned of its location while asking around earlier, so there was no need to ask for directions again.

Still, could things really be this absurd?

The dignified Minister of War of a prefecture didn't even go home at night, choosing instead to live in a temple?

_Why don't you just resign from your post already?_

Li Qi found it utterly ridiculous, but he still had a job to do. To withdraw the military rations, he absolutely needed this Minister of War's signature.

Looking at it this way, however, the man seemed incredibly unreliable. Would getting the signature truly go smoothly?

Burdened with worry, Li Qi knew the task had to be completed. Taking advantage of the fact that it wasn't yet midnight and the nightlife hadn't died down, he mounted Old Ma and picked up the pace through the city.

His speed was limited, however. Flying within the city was forbidden, under penalty of being shot down. Galloping wildly was equally impossible; the streets were choked with people enjoying the night market. The roads were basically jammed, and pedestrians frequently darted across the carriage lanes, making it difficult to gain any real speed.

Left with no other choice, he simply had Old Ma move as swiftly as it safely could.

Rushing as best he could, it took Li Qi about two quarters of an hour to finally exit the city. Once outside, his speed increased dramatically. Another quarter of an hour later, he arrived at Heavenly Peace Temple.

Strangely enough, although there were lights burning within the temple, it had no doors.

It lacked even a front gate, seemingly allowing anyone to come and go as they pleased.

Remembering that the monk Chanzhi was likely a high-level cultivator, and knowing for a fact that a Rank 7 Lord Minister of War was inside, Li Qi dismounted at the foot of the mountain. He left Old Ma to wait there while he shouldered the black cotton curtain and hiked up the mountain on foot.

Thanks to his Martial Dao cultivation base, his ascent was remarkably fast.

Along the way, he noticed numerous houses and farmlands. It seemed people had settled on this mountain year-round. What was once a barren hill was now heavily populated. However, as it was already night, there was no one wandering about outside, despite the glowing lights.

Li Qi took a brief look around but didn't linger, heading straight up the mountain.

Upon reaching the summit, Li Qi spotted a courtyard and... a pagoda.

The temple courtyard occupied a sizable area, containing seventeen or eighteen rooms and a grand hall. Situated directly behind the grand hall was a five-story pagoda towering two hundred feet into the air.

Li Qi swept his gaze over the area. Just as he prepared to step forward, he saw a gaunt, bald man approaching him from the courtyard.

The bald man possessed a kind face and benevolent eyes. He appeared to be in his sixties, his skin sagging and spotted with age, yet he carried himself with an energetic spirit.

Li Qi stepped forward, but before he could speak, the old monk took the initiative to greet him. "Almsgiver, to have traveled day and night and arrived at such a late hour, you must have urgent business. Are you here to seek the Minister of War of Vast Yang?"

Li Qi pondered for a moment. Believing that the Buddhism of this world might be entirely different from the Buddhism in the memories of his previous world, he opted not to rely on his preconceived notions. Instead, he asked honestly, "May I ask, esteemed elder... what exactly is an almsgiver?"

"An almsgiver refers to someone who has not left the secular world. Those who remain in their homes yet are willing to come to a temple to worship Buddha are all considered almsgivers. As for this poor monk, you may call me Chanzhi. Ah, but to my question—are you indeed here to find the Vast Yang Minister of War?"

Hearing this, Li Qi nodded and replied, "That is correct. I bear an urgent military report. I entreat Chanzhi... er, Grandmaster Chanzhi, to pass along word and have Lord Minister of War provide a swift response. The lives of over a hundred thousand people are at stake; there can be no delay."

He looked incredibly anxious, and the mention of over a hundred thousand lives made Chanzhi realize the gravity of the situation.

"The Vast Yang Minister of War is currently within that five-story pagoda, comprehending the Buddhist Dharma. If it is truly an emergency, please ascend at once, Almsgiver. No one will stop you," Monk Chanzhi said, pointing at the pagoda in the courtyard.

"Many thanks, Grandmaster." Having received permission, Li Qi immediately rushed toward the five-story pagoda.

However, upon reaching the pagoda, he discovered that it was no ordinary structure.

"A dharma treasure..." Li Qi frowned as he sensed the qi radiating from it.

This pagoda was actually a dharma treasure, though its specific function remained unknown to him.

He carefully inspected the structure. It was entirely made of wood, its construction loose and haphazard. It looked as if it had been pieced together, plank by ordinary plank, in a shoddy manner that left it appearing perilously unstable.

However, the human qi of ten thousand households, the Buddhist light, and numerous other types of qi that Li Qi couldn't identify were all mingled together. This amalgamation imbued the seemingly ramshackle pagoda with an extraordinary sense of opulence.

To an ordinary person, this object was nothing more than a dilapidated wooden tower—and a very poorly constructed one at that.

But Li Qi, as a shaman, possessed the ability to observe the flow of qi within all things in the world.

This intuitive perception was something other Daoist tradition lacked; it was an ability unique to the Shaman Dao.

Take death qi and baleful qi for instance. A practitioner of the Martial Dao could sense them through a sixth sense; they wouldn't see anything there, but they could detect the presence of the baleful qi.

But Li Qi could see the black and red qi in that location with his naked eyes.

The same applied to things like the military qi gathered by a massive army.

Other Daoist traditions could only perceive it by assessing the troops' discipline, their battle standards, and their general imposing aura. But in the eyes of a shaman, military qi actually coalesced into a physical entity that hovered above the army formation, taking on various distinct shapes.

When the Daoist Sects laid down formations and channeled the qi of heaven and earth, they constantly used yin-yang numerology to calculate the positions of various types of qi. They relied on assorted methods to deduce the qi's location. But a shaman didn't need to deduce anything; the qi was simply right there in front of them.

It was incredibly intuitive.

It was much like radio waves. Mortals could not see them and had to rely on instruments to detect their presence, but shamans could look directly at them.

This was the very reason Li Qi had been able to intuitively perceive the situation in that small county town in Li Prefecture's Yi County early on.

And now, this ability manifested once again.

Majestic. Colossal. Unbelievable.

This tiny pagoda unveiled layers of boundless profundity before Li Qi's eyes.

This wooden tower brimmed with an immense, terrifying deterrence. It felt staggeringly massive, like a whale breaching the surface of the sea—a sight as beautiful as it was awe-inspiring.

Standing before this behemoth, Li Qi felt like a mere sardine swimming beside that whale. The majestic pressure buffeted his hair and clothes, forcing him to halt his sprint and stop dead in his tracks before the wooden tower.

This pagoda... seemed to be the projection of some higher entity.

Merely sensing a tiny fraction of its existence sent shivers down Li Qi's spine, rendering him unable to run. He felt utterly compelled to maintain at least a basic level of respect in its presence.

This intuitive perception of a shaman left him absolutely stunned by the overwhelming impact.

Sometimes, intuitive perception was not a blessing.

While others might merely find the structure somewhat imposing, Li Qi felt as if he had been pinned beneath a mountain, completely immobilized.

Therefore, he stopped before the wooden tower, pressed his palms together, bent at the waist, and offered an earnest bow.

_Whoosh—_

It felt as though a great mountain had been lifted from his shoulders.

Li Qi was drenched in cold sweat, feeling as if he had plunged into an icy abyss.

_What... what is this thing?_

A wooden tower. A dharma artifact. Both were perfectly understandable. Even the human qi of ten thousand households contained within was comprehensible.

But what in the world was this oppressive pressure?

Li Qi couldn't even put words to what it was.

But to his senses, it felt as though... hundreds of billions of stars were held within a single palm. The endless universe itself was unable to contain its form. Free, eternal, everlasting—a unique coercive aura that pierced directly through the entirety of 'space and time' from bottom to top.

He clearly understood that this pagoda possessed no such capability. It was all merely his 'hallucination.'

Li Qi was simply perceiving some sort of essence concealed within it.

It was as if he were a maggot that had glimpsed a tiny ray of sunlight, and by tracing that path of light backward and lifting his head, he had finally laid eyes upon the sun itself.

Fortunately, he was a shaman. Under that sun, he was at least a maggot with eyes. If he were a Rank 9 practitioner of any other Daoist tradition, he would likely be a completely blind maggot—only able to feel the warm light, entirely oblivious to the terror hidden behind that warmth.

However, no matter how terrifying it was, he still had to go inside.

The lives of over a hundred thousand people were in his hands. Even if it was a mountain of blades or a sea of fire, he had to wade through it.

Stowing away any trace of disrespect, Li Qi advanced with measured steps and solemnly entered the pagoda.

Stepping onto the first floor, he saw a plaque hanging above that read: "Arhat Hall."

Inside, one hundred and eight Arhat statues were enshrined. Placed beside the Arhat statues were devilish entities bowing low in submission, as if staging a scene of Arhats subduing devils.

A large painted drum was set on the right side of the hall, and a bell hung on the left, radiating a solemn and dignified majesty.

Li Qi wove his way through the one hundred and eight Arhats. Some wore expressions of wrath, others of compassion, tranquility, or devil-conquering fury. They reclined or leaned in entirely different postures, yet every single one was incredibly lifelike. Walking amongst them felt like being scrutinized by one hundred and eight sets of eyes simultaneously. If one harbored a guilty conscience, they likely wouldn't even make it past this path.

Li Qi felt as though those gazes were piercing right through his flesh, uncovering every last secret buried in his heart, invoking an irresistible sense of dread, and even frantic madness.

A person carrying guilt in their heart would probably be driven by the sudden urge to smash every Arhat statue in the room.

Fortunately, Li Qi conducted himself with righteousness and treated others with justice. Beneath those two virtues, his heart was open and clear. Even under such intense scrutiny, he remained unbothered, strolling leisurely as he crossed the first floor.

He made his way up to the second floor.

The second floor also bore a plaque, reading: "Bodhisattva Hall."

Upon reaching this floor, he saw ten Bodhisattva statues.

The moment he entered, Li Qi felt a solemn aura wash over him. Despite them being lifeless objects, they exuded a distinct presence...

Some embodied a Zen heart like the bright moon, the wondrous source of Prajna.

Some reflected a natural, clear moon in the water, a Dharma drum gathering fish and dragons.

Some represented the protection of divine might.

And some offered the merit of water in a glazed vase, revealing auspiciousness in tasseled shrines and clouds.

He saw the many Bodhisattva statues, shrouded in the rich fragrance of incense that condensed into canopies. With spiraled hair buns adorned in tassels and feet resting upon lotus blossoms, they appeared majestic yet remarkably gentle, lacking the oppressive weight of the Arhat statues below.

Li Qi walked through this area with relative ease. Although it was solemn, he encountered no actual resistance.

When he reached the third floor, there were no Buddha statues. Instead, books were laid out, accompanied by a plaque that read: "Sutra Altar."

Sutra desks and incense basins were arranged here, holding several hundred volumes that seemingly invited anyone to flip through them at will.

By the time he reached this point, the sense of oppression had largely faded.

Li Qi's inner bookworm was roused. He stepped up to a sutra desk, intent on reading a passage or two. But on second thought, he realized that reading these texts might lead him to question his own Dao path.

Classics. As mentioned before, only the classics contained teachings of the "Dao." The philosophies, the core essence, the pursuits, and the fundamental nature of a given cultivation path could only be found detailed within the classics.

If one read too much of this sort of material, it was easy to be led astray by another tradition. It offered no benefit to early-stage cultivation. Only later, after forging one's own distinct path, would reading these to draw analogies hold any real meaning.

Furthermore, these were the classics of another sect. Flipping through them without permission was a severe breach of taboo.

Thus, he suppressed his bookworm instincts and headed upstairs once more.

Arriving at the fourth floor, he found a plaque reading: "Hall of the Three Buddhas."

Three colossal Buddha statues were enshrined within the hall.

The three Buddhas possessed identical appearances, looking like the exact same person. There was barely any discernible difference in their faces, yet their postures varied wildly.

One Buddha wore a cassock of clouds, leaned upon a bamboo staff, and held Sanskrit sutras bound in palm leaves across his lap. This Buddha was incredibly old and weathered, its paint already peeling off.

Another Buddha hugged its knees and hunched its shoulders. It resembled a Ghost King—inexplicably terrifying, as if it sought to devour all others. This Buddha was spotlessly new, looking as though it had just been completed.

The final Buddha sat with eyes closed, withering away amidst ten thousand mountains, its long eyebrows sweeping the floor. This Buddha was not yet finished; it was merely a clay mold.

Looking at these three Buddhas made Li Qi's skin crawl.

He confirmed it with a single glance: this was the 'sun' he had sensed!

Just behind these Buddha statues lay an indescribable magnificence. It was as if all things were severed here, then pulverized, and ultimately merged together to form an expanse of 'boundless light' that pierced through all space and time.

Standing before it, Li Qi swallowed hard.

Then, without a moment's hesitation, he scrambled up to the fifth floor.
